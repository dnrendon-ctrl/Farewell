# Digital Farewell App (v1): Recipient-Owned Export-First Keepsake

## Product Overview

Digital farewell app where collaborators write goodbye messages, attach photos, and coordinate gifts around a recipient’s move or major life transition. The core wedge is personal farewells for friends/family during moving away or major life transitions; biggest reason current attempts fail: “messages and photos scattered in chat can’t be preserved into one meaningful keepsake.” v1 ships as a web PWA that creates a tokenized invitation, collects contributions, and generates a recipient-owned private export page controlled by the recipient. The product uses an export-first 14-day free trial: the recipient gets 14 days of free unlimited exports on first invite, while collaborators contribute as usual.
## Problem & Opportunity

People coordinate farewells in chats (text threads and group messages), then struggle to preserve everything in one place after the transition. “Messages and photos scattered in chat can’t be preserved into one meaningful keepsake,” so the outcome users want (a lasting artifact they can keep and share later) gets lost in scattered media. Farewell cards and group sign tools exist, but they don’t reliably preserve the mix of text + photos in an owner-controlled export format. There’s room for a privacy-preserving, recipient-owned keepsake that turns contributions into a downloadable artifact instead of a perpetually-lived chat log.
## Target Customer

Primary ICP: people who are leaving (moving, graduating, job change, relocation) and want a meaningful farewell from friends/family—typically sending a single invite link to a small-to-mid group (friends, relatives, coworkers). They care about privacy and final ownership: the recipient should control what gets saved, when it’s downloaded, and what access collaborators have. They’re not trying to run a public event; they want a clean, minimal workflow and an export that survives beyond the day-to-day chat. Explicitly out of scope for V1: users who need full real-time collaboration (editing after submission, live layout changes), large-scale communities, and gift ordering/checkout flows.
## Value Proposition

We convert distributed messages/photos into one recipient-controlled keepsake export page. The differentiator is the export-first artifact model: collaborators contribute to a specific farewell, but the final export is owned by the recipient and remains downloadable on the paid/unlocked path. Switching trigger reduces friction: the recipient gets 14 days of free unlimited exports when opening the first invite, so they can confirm the value before paying. The user gets a “preview-first premium feel” without subscriptions in v1: pay once for the unlock to download/save the final export after the trial window.
## Competitive Landscape

Direct alternatives include Thankbox, GroupGreeting, Punchbowl, and GroupTogether—each supports signed messages and often photo-like media, but typically lacks a recipient-owned private export model that cleanly preserves contributions into a single lasting artifact under the recipient’s control. The competitor gap is the experience after the moment passes: chat-based coordination breaks preservation (“messages and photos scattered in chat can’t be preserved into one meaningful keepsake”). The do-nothing option is continuing in chat and manually saving screenshots/photos, which creates a fragmented record that’s hard to share later. If competitors ship earlier with privacy-forward export ownership or an export-first trial/payment trigger, they can capture the “first artifact” moment that drives retention and word-of-mouth.
## Core Capabilities & Platform

Essential capabilities for v1: (1) recipient creates a farewell and shares a tokenized invite link, (2) collaborators add text messages and photo attachments to that farewell, (3) recipient sees an export-ready preview and generates a private recipient-owned export page, (4) recipient unlock/payment controls download/save access, and (5) gift coordination via a simple checklist link (no ordering/payment). Platform choice is web PWA first because the target behavior is link-based sharing with mobile-friendly photo upload and a quick path to “view/export/download” from any device. We explicitly do not commit to deep real-time collaboration inside the shared export page beyond minimal day-one support: v1 boundary is “minimal real-time collaboration inside shared export page with photo uploads and instant export generation (day one).” We also avoid live editing after submission, ordering/checkout, and complex gift inventory.
## Design Style References

The UI should feel minimal and clean with one theme, simple photo placement, and form-based message entry (short text, optional photo). Align with a “preview-first premium feel”: collaborators can submit quickly, while the recipient gets a clearly structured export page with a single download CTA. Avoid dense feed layouts and chat-like timelines on the export; the artifact should look like a keepsake page, not a conversation log. Accessibility requirement for the web app: WCAG 2.1 AA for contrast, focus states, keyboard navigation, and readable typography; mobile-first layout is mandatory.
## MVP & Phasing

MVP is end-to-end export creation: recipient creates farewell via web PWA, shares tokenized invite links, collaborators add goodbye messages + attach photos to that same farewell, and the recipient generates a private recipient-owned export page. Gifts coordination is a separate, simple checklist link the recipient can share; collaborators can tick items, but there’s no ordering/payment. We ship the export-first 14-day free trial exactly as follows: on opening the first invite, the recipient gets 14 days of free unlimited exports; collaborators contribute as usual; exports remain downloadable afterward. What we’re not building in v1: real-time editing of submitted content, multi-page complex layouts, ordering/payment, and subscription billing.

### Implementation Dependencies & Constraints (v1)

Export-first trial gating must be enforced for both “export generation” and “download/save final export.” Exports must remain accessible for download after the trial window, while “payment unlock” gates downloading/saving of final exports beyond the allowed trial behavior. Photo uploads need immediate persistence so the export can be generated on day one without waiting for background jobs that risk user-visible failure.
## Success Metrics

We track recurrence-led KPIs built from activation, with explicit targets for month 0–6. Primary activation metric: 20% of invite links produce completed export by day 14. Monetization metric: 10% of completed exports convert to paid unlocks. Recurrence metric: 30% of paying recipients complete a second farewell within 90 days.

### Measurement Details (what counts)

“Completed export by day 14” means recipient has generated the export page with all contributions available (including uploaded photos) and it’s in a completed status. “Paid unlock” is the one-time unlock payment after the free trial, based on the unlock record tied to recipient and the export/farewell. “Second farewell” is a separate farewell created by the same recipient and completed through export generation within 90 days.
## Go-to-Market & Business Model

Business model is $9.99 one-time unlock after the free trial; no subscription in v1. After day 14, new export creation remains unlimited, but downloading/saving the final export requires the $9.99 unlock; collaborators can contribute up to the paywall and do not need to pay. Early validation evidence target for the first release is willingness to pay for the artifact (export download/save) rather than willingness to join a chat—so the 14-day trial is designed to convert once value is proven by the recipient’s preview. First reach is via direct sharing loops: onboarding flow creates a tokenized invite link and gift checklist link that recipients can send to the exact circle of friends/family affected by the transition.
## Key Risks & Assumptions

Risk 1: the export-first trial doesn’t create urgency—if recipients never generate the export during the 14-day window, conversion drops. Invalidate/measure early by checking “export generated by day 3” and “preview viewed” rates; if low, shorten friction in generation and improve the preview UX. Risk 2: photo upload reliability and export generation latency—if uploads fail or exports take too long, collaborators churn and recipients lose trust; mitigate with resumable uploads, clear progress UI, and server-side validation. Risk 3: privacy/permissions confusion—collaborators must not see private export download state; mitigate with strict backend authorization checks and simple client-side states. Risk 4: market overlap with competitors—if competitors’ products provide better ownership or retention mechanics first, we may need to emphasize the recipient-owned private export model and download/save control as the core differentiator.
